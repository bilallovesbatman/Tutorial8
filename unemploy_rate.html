1 Executive Summary

This study uses a linear regression model and correlation analysis to examine the relationship between cash rates and unemployment in Australia from 2013 to 2023. The analysis reveals a trade-off between these two macroeconomic indicators, with the government closely monitoring employment levels. It can be seen clearly during the beginning of the Covid-19 pandemic in 2019-2020. There was a significant increase in the unemployment rate while the cash rate decreased conversely. This study can confirm the well-known trade-off relationship between cash rates and unemployment; further research is required to understand the factors influencing unemployment rates in the country. Exploring other economic indicators and conducting more complex statistical analyses could provide a better understanding of the relationships between these variables.

2 Introduction

This study evaluates the impact of cash rate changes on Australias unemployment rates. The motivation behind this research is that these two macroeconomic indicators are crucial for policymakers to achieve economic growth and stability targets in the critical role that cash rate changes and unemployment rates play in shaping the Australian economy. Understanding the relationship between these indicators can inform the development of effective policies and strategies to manage the Australian economy. Data from 2013 to 2023 will present the result, covering and reflecting economic challenges such as the COVID-19 pandemic. Moreover, The findings will help people understand the correlation between two variables among operations in the Australian economy.

6 Results

  • According to the result of The linear regression model from the table 4.1, the intercept value is 5.35668, indicating that when the cash rate is 0, the expected average unemployment rate is 5.35668. The coefficient for the cash rate predictor variable is 0.05452, which means that for every unit increase in the cash rate, the expected unemployment rate will increase by 0.05452, assuming all other variables are held constant. The standard error for the coefficient estimate is 0.14423, indicating the variability of the estimated coefficient. The t-value of 0.378 and the p-value of 0.707 for the cash rate variable suggest that it is not statistically significant in predicting the unemployment rate, as the p-value is more significant than the significance level of 0.05.

7 Conclusion

In conclusion, we can see a trade-off between cash rates and unemployment in Australia. We can predict that the government would closely monitor the employment level, as it is a reliable indicator of the economys performance. For example, if unemployment increases, the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA) may decide to lower the interest rates to boost spending to encourage businesses and individuals to borrow and spend more, stimulating economic growth and creating new job opportunities. On the other hand, if inflation is becoming a concern, the RBA may increase the cash rate to slow down spending and borrowing, which can also help keep the economy stable in the long run. To clarify, the analysis conducted confirms the trade-off relationship between cash rates and unemployment, as is well-known in economic theory. However, the correlation between the two variables must be more vital to draw definitive conclusions about their relationship. It is important to note that correlation does not necessarily imply causation and additional analysis would be necessary to establish any causal relationships between these variables. Therefore, while the linear regression and correlation analyses provide some insights into the relationship between cash rates and average unemployment rates in Australia, further research is needed to understand the factors influencing unemployment rates in the country entirely. This could involve exploring other economic indicators, such as inflation rates, GDP growth, and industry-specific data, and conducting more complex statistical analyses to model the relationships between these variables better.
